# Counting Rhythm

Musicians need to develop a strong rhythmic sense, and several practice techniques help build it without forcing playing to become mechanical.

## Tap the beat while counting out loud

Tap the beat as you count out loud during practice. You can play with one hand at a time and tap with the other.

## Use a metronome selectively

Use a metronome some but not all of the time to give you the literal placement of the beats in “clock time.” It anchors timing but should not become a crutch.

## Verbalize the subdivisions

When eighth and sixteenth notes are difficult, treat them as rhythmic subdivisions of the beat. Concentrate on the beat, and verbalize the subdivisions aloud.

For example, in 3/4 meter with eighth note subdivisions, count “1 & 2 & 3 &.” For 3/4 meter with sixteenth notes, count “1 e & a 2 e & a 3 e & a.”

## Embody the rhythm — clap and conduct

Tap or clap your hands together as you do these exercises. This helps you embody the rhythm, making it a physiological activity rather than mere points in time.

Another way to embody rhythm is to learn basic conducting patterns for simple meter — one of the most powerful techniques instrumentalists can use to strengthen rhythmic skills.
