# Rules to rectify wrong notes problem

## We ARE what we PRACTICE
This means that through practice we get good at what we are practicing. If you practice the wrong thing, you get good at doing the wrong thing. If you practice a piece, playing a wrong note, correcting that wrong note and going on, you get very good at playing that wrong note, correcting it and going on. And that’s NOT what you want.

## Psychology of musical performance
You know where that wrong note is going to come up, and you're thinking about it way in advance. You can hear it clearly and you say to yourself, I WILL NOT play this wrong note. Then you do! Rats! What could be wrong. You warned yourself about it over and over, yet it happened again.

I’m now going to talk a bit about the psychology of musical performance. Some of it might sound like voodoo, but don’t knock it ’til you’ve thoughtfully tried it. Bear this in mind: your fingers are controlled by your motor nervous system. Your motor nervous system is controlled by your brain but it is not that part of the brain that makes value judgments. Your motor nervous system only knows what your are thinking about. It does not know whether you want it or not. It cannot understand negative thoughts. You think negative thoughts, and your motor neurons say to each other: “OK gang, that’s what he/she wants, let’s go get it!” You will get what you are thinking about, whether you want it or not. So you must be constantly conceptualizing what you do want your audience to hear.

## Mechanical Approach
Alright, if you don’t care for the “voodoo” (although you cannot play well without it!), this part will be more mechanical. When you play a wrong note, the problem is NOT the wrong note itself. It is in the interval with which you approach that note. At the very least, you must study your approach to that note from the last “right” note. This is a little easier to discuss regarding a single-line instrument like trumpet. Are you observing the fingerings provided? If there are none or if the printed ones don’t work for you, you’ll have to work them out yourself. If there are other notes being played by the same hand, they of course must be part of the solution. And bear in mind that the solution to one problem may simply move the problem upstream. I can envision a solution where you have to chase a fingering problem all the way back to the beginning of the piece! But do establish a very comfortable approach to what was the wrong note.

## The Power of Repetition
Think about how you practice. If you play something wrong four or five times, finally get it right, heave a sigh of relief, and proceed onward, what are your fingers going to remember tomorrow - the way you practiced it four or five times, or the way you practiced it once? Once. you get it right, repeat that at least more times than you have played it wrong. Bear in mind that this rule has nothing to do with tempo. Slow down as much as it takes to get it right. Once the correct behavioral patterns are established, you can always speed them up. But gradually! Let your body (fingers, arms, shoulders, back…) learn at their own speed. You will accomplish nothing by getting impatient with your own body. To promote your own growth, first of all, you must accept what is - right now.

## The art of graceful recovery
In our practice, we are inclined to stop for errors and correct them - always!!!!!!! But in performance that is not a useful process. Can you overlook a mistake and keep going without losing continuity? We always presume “yes”, but we practice “no”. And we get really good at it. So now you’re on stage in front of an audience. Has your practice prepared you to deal artistically with the inevitable mistake? For most students, I think not.

Finally - back to the voodoo. There is no voodoo in communicating to your audience exactly what you need to say about the piece your are playing. And if that idealized concept of the piece is not foremost on your mind as you perform it, how can you possibly expect to communicate it!
